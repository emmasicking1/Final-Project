Early Women’s Athletics at MSJ

Women’s athletics at Mount St. Joseph began with limited opportunities, but strong interest from athletes. As Jean Dowell explained, there was “never a lack of interest, but a lack of opportunity” for women’s sports.

Early programs included basketball and volleyball, with basketball in its first year and volleyball already beginning to grow. Athletes were recruited through organizations like the Catholic Youth Organization (CYO), which helped bring talented players to the Mount.

Resources were limited. Teams traveled by bus, and the program was influenced by the structure of the all-women’s college, which was run by the Sisters of Charity. Despite this, strong athletes and coaches helped build a competitive foundation.

Growth of Women’s Sports

The expansion of women’s athletics in the late 1960s and 1970s marked a major turning point. New sports such as swimming, tennis, and track and field were added, creating more opportunities for female athletes.

According to Dowell, the Mount attracted “amazing athletes,” even in the early years. As programs grew, the university became known for the strength of its women’s athletics.

The Impact of Title IX

Although women’s athletics at MSJ began before Title IX, the law significantly accelerated growth and recognition. Title IX “changed everything,” according to Dowell, by ensuring greater support and legitimacy for women’s sports.

Before Title IX, there were no legal protections guaranteeing equal opportunities for women in athletics. After its passage in 1972, programs became more structured, competitive, and supported.
