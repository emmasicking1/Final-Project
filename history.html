The Impact of Title IX

Title IX, passed in 1972, mandated gender equity in education, requiring that schools receiving federal funding provide equal opportunities for men and women, including in athletics.

This meant that women’s athletic programs needed to be on par with men’s programs in terms of funding, facilities, and support. However, the experience at Mount St. Joseph University was unique.

Because the Mount was originally an all-women’s college, it did not immediately benefit from Title IX in the same way as other institutions. With no men’s sports programs to compare against, there was no direct imbalance to correct.

As John Spinney explained, there “couldn’t have been disparity” before the introduction of men’s athletics. However, once MSJ became coeducational in 1986 and began adding men’s sports in the early 1990s, Title IX became essential in ensuring that women’s programs remained equally supported.

Leaders such as Jean Dowell and others played a critical role in maintaining this balance, advocating for fairness, and ensuring that women’s athletics continued to receive the recognition and resources they deserved.
