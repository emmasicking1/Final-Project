Early Women’s Athletics at MSJ

Women’s athletics at Mount St. Joseph University developed in a unique environment. As an all-women’s college, athletics were highly valued and respected from the beginning.

Early programs included basketball, volleyball, and field hockey. Despite limited resources, strong interest and dedication helped build competitive teams.

As Jean Dowell explained, there was “never a lack of interest, but a lack of opportunity” for women’s sports.

Growth and Change

Over time, the Mount expanded its athletic programs to include sports such as swimming, tennis, and track and field. These additions created more opportunities for female athletes and strengthened the program.

The university became known for its strong women’s athletics, attracting talented athletes and building a reputation for competitiveness.

The Impact of Title IX

Title IX, passed in 1972, required schools receiving federal funding to provide equal opportunities for men and women in athletics.

Because MSJ was originally an all-women’s school, it did not immediately experience the same impact as other institutions. There were no men’s teams to compare against.

Once the university became coeducational in 1986 and added men’s sports in the early 1990s, Title IX became essential in ensuring fairness between programs.
