The Impact of Title IX

Title IX, passed in 1972, is a federal law that prohibits discrimination based on sex in educational programs that receive federal funding, including athletics.

Under Title IX, schools are required to provide equal athletic opportunities for men and women. This includes areas such as participation, resources, facilities, and overall treatment, rather than requiring identical funding or programs.

The impact of Title IX at Mount St. Joseph University was unique. Because the Mount was originally an all-women’s college, there were no men’s teams to compare against. As a result, the university did not initially experience the same type of changes seen at coeducational institutions.

According to John Spinney, there “couldn’t have been disparity” before the introduction of men’s athletics. This highlights how the effects of Title IX depended on each institution’s structure and history.

When MSJ became coeducational in 1986 and began adding men’s sports in the early 1990s, Title IX became much more significant. At that point, the university had to ensure that both men’s and women’s programs were supported equitably.

Leaders such as Jean Dowell played an important role in maintaining this balance, advocating for fairness and helping ensure that women’s athletics continued to receive strong support as the program expanded.

Overall, Title IX did not impact all institutions in the same way. At MSJ, its importance grew over time, particularly after the transition to a coeducational environment.
